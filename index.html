About the Trostenets

Maly Trostenets was the largest Nazi extermination camp on the territory of Belarus and one of the deadliest in Europe. It was located about 12 kilometers from Minsk, near the village of Maly Trostenets.

The camp operated from spring 1942 until July 1944, during the Nazi occupation of Belarus.

Trostenets was not a typical concentration camp. Its primary purpose was mass murder.

Victims were:

  • shot,
  • killed in gas vans (mobile gas chambers),
  • burned in mass graves and open-air pyres.

This memorial honors the memory of the children who suffered and died under Nazi crimes and stands as a reminder of the tragic impact of war.

Those murdered at Trostenets included:

  • Jews from Minsk and the Minsk Ghetto,
  • Jews deported from Germany, Austria, Czechoslovakia, and Poland,
  • Soviet prisoners of war,
  • partisans and resistance members,
  • civilians.

Estimates of the number of victims range from 60,000 to over 200,000 people. Many historians today consider around 200,000 victims to be the most likely figure. This makes Trostenets the fourth-largest Nazi extermination site in Europe, after Auschwitz, Treblinka, and Belzec.

Trostenets is now recognized as a major site of the Holocaust and Nazi crimes in Eastern Europe and serves as a powerful reminder of the scale of civilian suffering during World War II.
